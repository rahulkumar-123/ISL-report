2. Module 1: Drones

We learned about Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs), how to build them, and how they fly by themselves. This module was very hands-on and provided a good foundation for the rest of the internship.

Building a Drone

We started by building a quadcopter from a kit. This helped us see how all the parts work together and why each component is important. It was a good lesson in mechanical assembly and electronics.

  • Put together the carbon fiber frame and mounted the brushless DC motors, making sure everything was balanced to reduce vibration.
  • Wired up the electronics, including the Electronic Speed Controllers (ESCs), the Power Distribution Board (PDB), and the flight controller.
  • Learned about battery safety and proper handling procedures for high-discharge LiPo batteries.
  • Used a computer with ground control software to set up the drone's firmware and calibrate the Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) and compass.

How Drones Fly Autonomously

We also learned about the software side of drones. It was important to test the code in a simulator before trying it on the real drone to ensure it would be safe.

  • Learned how drones use sensors like GPS and IMU to know where they are, and how sensor fusion algorithms combine this data.
  • Studied how PID controllers are tuned to keep the drone stable in the air, preventing it from wobbling or overshooting its target.
  • Looked at how drones can follow a path on a map using a series of GPS waypoints, which are sent using protocols like MAVLink.

3. Module 2: CanSats

This module was about building a small satellite that fits in a can. We did this as a team project, which meant we had to work together, manage our time, and solve problems to get it done.

Project KIRAN

Our team had to build a CanSat to measure background radiation with a Geiger counter. The idea was to see if we could detect changes in radiation as it fell from a height, simulating a probe entering an area with high radiation, like near a solar storm.

Project Info

  • Goal: Measure radiation (counts per minute) and send the data to a computer on the ground in real-time.
  • My Job: I worked on the Geiger counter payload. This meant I had to get the sensor working, manage its high-voltage power supply, and connect it to the main computer.
  • Problem: The radio signals from the transmitter were creating electromagnetic interference (EMI) that was messing up the very sensitive sensor readings, giving us false high counts of radiation.
  • Solution: We first tried to solve it with software, but it wasn't enough. So, we wrapped the sensor in some copper foil to act as a simple shield, which blocks some of the radio waves. I also changed the code to pause the sensor readings for the few milliseconds when the radio was transmitting. This combination fixed the problem and our data became much cleaner and more reliable.

4. Module 3: Rockets

This part was about the basics of how rockets work to get to space. We covered the main ideas of propulsion and flight dynamics.

Rocket Science Basics

  • Learned about the main rocket equation (Tsiolkovsky rocket equation) and what it means for fuel, understanding that a rocket's mass is mostly fuel.
  • Compared solid and liquid fuel rockets and their pros and cons, like how solid rockets are simple but liquid rockets are more efficient.
  • Studied how to make rockets fly straight using fins by ensuring the center of pressure is behind the center of gravity.
  • Talked about why large rockets need multiple stages to get to orbit, dropping empty fuel tanks to save weight.

Model Rocket Workshop

We built and launched a small model rocket. It was a good way to see the science in action. Before the launch, we used simulation software like OpenRocket to predict how high it would go and to make sure it would be stable. We had to follow a checklist to make sure the launch was safe. The rocket went up and the parachute came out for a safe recovery, so it was a success.

5. Module 4: GIS

Here, we learned how to use satellite images and GIS software to study the Earth. GIS stands for Geographic Information System and it's a powerful tool for analyzing location-based data.

Case Study: Wildfire Risk

We used QGIS software to look at wildfire risk in a forest area in the Himalayas. The goal was to find the most dangerous areas based on a few factors that we could measure from satellite images.

Steps

  • Used NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) to see how many plants and trees were in an area, which acts as the fuel for a fire.
  • Used NDMI (Normalized Difference Moisture Index) to see how dry the plants were, as drier plants burn more easily.
  • Looked at the slope of the land using a digital elevation model (DEM), because fires spread faster uphill.
  • We combined these data layers in QGIS to create a final risk map with high, medium, and low risk zones.
  • Result: We made a map showing where fires were most likely to happen, which could be useful for forest managers to plan fire prevention efforts.

6. Module 5: Space Industry

This module was about the business side of space and jobs in the industry. It helped connect our technical skills to the real world and showed us what careers are possible.

India's Space Startups

We talked about how private companies are getting more involved in space in India, working with ISRO. We learned about the role of IN-SPACe, a government agency created to help and promote private space companies. This is a big change for the country's space program and is creating new opportunities for startups.

Jobs in Space

We learned that there are many different jobs in the space industry, not just for aerospace engineers. People are also needed in areas like data science to analyze satellite images, robotics for rovers and manufacturing, and even law, finance, and marketing for space companies.
